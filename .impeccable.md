## Design Context

### Users
- **Primary builder**: Developer who built the app — understands finance and tech deeply
- **Household co-manager**: Girlfriend — manages shared finances, needs clarity not jargon
- **Occasional user**: Daughter — least financially experienced, needs the most accessible interface
- **Context**: Used at home, on phones and laptops, to answer "what can we spend?" and "where did our money go?"
- **Jobs to be done**: Check daily spending allowance, review budget status, understand spending patterns, read AI-generated insights and reports

### Brand Personality
**Warm, clear, trustworthy**

- Feels like a well-organized family notebook, not a bank dashboard
- Evokes calm confidence about money — never anxiety or pressure
- "Cashbird" suggests lightness and freedom — the design should feel airy, not heavy
- The interface should make money feel manageable, even when budgets are tight
- Anti-references: Bloomberg terminals, corporate banking apps, SaaS dashboards with navy/gold palettes, dark mode finance bros

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Visual tone**: Clean and warm — light mode primary, soft warm neutrals instead of cold grays
- **Theme**: Light mode. Warm-tinted backgrounds (cream/sand, not pure white or cold gray-50)
- **Colors**: Earth-warm palette inspired by the "bird" motif — think amber, warm greens, soft terracotta. Avoid cold blues, neon accents, and the corporate finance navy/gold trap
- **Typography**: Friendly and readable — a display font with character for headings, a highly legible body font. Not monospace, not overly geometric
- **Icons**: Phosphor Icons — regular weight for nav, bold for emphasis
- **Layout**: Generous spacing, clear grouping, asymmetric where it creates emphasis. Not everything in cards
- **Motion**: Subtle — gentle fades and slides on page load, nothing bouncy or distracting

### Design Principles
1. **Clarity over cleverness** — Every label, number, and action should be immediately understandable by someone with no finance background
2. **Warmth over formality** — This is a family tool. The tone should feel like a helpful friend explaining your finances, not a bank statement
3. **Hierarchy of urgency** — The most actionable information ("what can I spend today?") should dominate. Secondary analysis is available but subordinate
4. **Progressive complexity** — Simple surface, depth on demand. Show the headline number first, let users drill into details if they want
5. **Trust through transparency** — When showing calculations or AI insights, make it clear where numbers come from. No black boxes with money
